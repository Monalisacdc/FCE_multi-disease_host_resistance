 Functional clustering of epidemic curves for multi-disease host resistance profiling: a case study for three foliar wheat diseases Monalisa Cristina De Col (1,2) , Edson Giovanni Kochinski (3) , Ronaldo S. Ortiz (3) , Helio Antonio Wood Joris (3) , Erick De Wolf (1) , Emerson Medeiros Del Ponte (2) (1) Department of Plant Pathology, Kansas State University, Manhattan, Kansas; (2) Departamento de Fitopatologia, Universidade Federal de Viçosa, Viçosa, Brazil; (3) Fundação ABC, Castro, Paraná, Brazil Corresponding author: Emerson M. Del Ponte ( delponte@ufv.br ) Abstract Wheat leaf rust (LR), powdery mildew (PM), and tan spot (TS) are among the most important foliar fungal diseases affecting wheat. Genetic resistance is key for disease management, and cultivars are commonly classified based on disease measurements taken at a single time-point. Disease progress curves (DPCs) provide richer data by capturing multiple components of partial resistance. We evaluated a functional clustering approach based on DPCs to characterize resistant phenotypes and identify wheat cultivars with resistance to multiple foliar diseases. Multi-environment assessments of LR, PM, and TS across six municipalities in Paraná and Sao Paulo State, Brazil, and multiple years (2019–25) included 25 cultivars. A hierarchical beta-regression generalized additive model was fitted to Zadoks-anchored DPC data, and functional similarity among cultivars was quantified using trajectory-based distances and hierarchical clustering. Epidemic descriptors, including the growth stage that the disease reached 5% severity ( t 5 ) and maximum epidemic velocity ( v max ), were derived. Multi-disease resistance (MDR) was assessed using a MDR index and Pareto front analysis. Agreement with final severity classification was low for TS (Adjusted Rand Index = 0.36) and higher for LR (ARI = 0.76) and PM (ARI = 0.91). Susceptible cultivars showed earlier epidemic onset and higher v max . Disease severity at key stages explaining functional classification were Zadoks 65–75 (TS), after Zadoks 80 (LR), and 70–80 (PM). One third of cultivars exhibited balanced resistance across diseases and were Pareto-optimal, whereas the others were resistant to only one or two diseases. Keywords : Disease progress curves, plant breeding, disease resistance phenotype. Introduction Wheat ( Triticum aestivum ) provides a substantial share of dietary protein and daily caloric intake for much of the global population (FAOSTAT, 2025), making it one of the most important crops worldwide. However, its production is continually challenged by a wide range of biotic stresses that reduce both yield potential and grain quality. Among these, fungal foliar diseases are particularly significant due to their widespread occurrence and capacity to cause severe epidemics under favorable environmental conditions (Kolmer, 2005; McCallum et al., 2012; Singh et al., 2016; Savary et al., 2017; Gupta et al., 2018; Savary et al., 2019). The rust pathogens ( Puccinia spp.) and powdery mildew ( Blumeria graminis f. sp. tritici ) have historically been among the most important diseases of wheat and remain persistent threats despite the extensive deployment of resistant cultivars and fungicide applications (Huerta-Espino et al., 2011; Singh et al., 2016; Singh et al., 2025). Among the rust diseases, leaf rust (LR), caused by Puccinia triticina , is the most prevalent globally and has been identified as the leading cause of yield losses in wheat production worldwide (Bolton et al., 2008; Savary et al., 2019). In parallel, other foliar diseases have gained prominence in specific regions. In many wheat-growing areas, particularly in South America, tan spot (TS), caused by Pyrenophora tritici-repentis , is currently associated with some of the highest yield reductions (Moreno et al., 2015; Moffat and Santana 2018; Savary et al., 2019). Powdery mildew (PM) continues to negatively influence wheat production, especially under conducive environmental conditions. In Brazil, yield losses attributed to PM have been reported to range from approximately 10% in cultivars with low resistance to as high as 62% in highly susceptible cultivars (Linhares, 1988; Reis et al., 1997), with average losses estimated between 5% and 8% across growing seasons (Cunfer, 2002). However, most of these estimates are based on outdated studies and may not fully reflect contemporary wheat varieties and production systems, particularly given the advances and changes in production practices over the past 20 years. In Brazil, recent information on yield losses caused by PM remains limited, likely because the disease has been relatively well controlled for an extended period. Since around 2015, the disease has re-emerged as a major concern, with increased prevalence and epidemic intensity, likely driven by mild and humid winters and the widespread adoption of susceptible cultivars (Kloppe et al., 2022). Collectively, these three foliar diseases highlight the complexity and variability of disease pressure in wheat systems and underscore the need for robust approaches to characterize and compare resistance across pathosystems. Plant host resistance represents the most economical, safe, and effective strategy for managing wheat diseases and is the most widely adopted approach in wheat production systems (Aktar-Uz-Zaman et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2021; Dinglasan et al., 2021). Genotype performance is not determined solely by genetic resistance, as phenotypic responses to disease vary across environments due to genotype-by-environment interactions, including variation in pathogen populations, which constitute an important component of the environmental context, particularly in terms of differences in virulence and aggressiveness (Francki et al., 2020). In this context, the relative contribution of genetic and environmental factors to disease resistance may differ among pathosystems. For example, resistance to TS can be strongly influenced by environmental conditions, with genetic effects explaining a smaller proportion of the observed variation, whereas resistance to other diseases may exhibit substantially higher heritability (Jighly et al., 2016). Beyond environmental influences, the way plant host resistance is quantified also plays an important role in how genotypic responses are interpreted. Resistance to foliar diseases in wheat is traditionally classified using scalar measures such as final percent severity, the area under the disease progress curve (AUDPC), or ordinal resistance categories assigned by breeders (Zhang et al., 2007; Losert et al., 2017). These metrics have long been used in plant disease epidemiology and breeding because they provide operationally convenient summaries of epidemic intensity (Madden et al., 2007). However, scalar summaries inherently reduce epidemic dynamics to single-point descriptors and may obscure biologically meaningful differences in how epidemics unfold over time (Seidel et al., 2026; Del Ponte, 2026). Disease progress curves capture multiple components of partial resistance, including delays in epidemic onset, reduced epidemic velocity, and differences in the overall shape of epidemic trajectories (Campbell and Madden, 1990; Madden et al., 2007). Despite this informational richness, resistance classification rarely exploits the full epidemic trajectory as a phenotypic object. Instead, due to cost constraints, evaluations typically rely on single assessments or aggregated scalar measures, leaving unresolved whether simplified field assessments at selected growth stages truly reflect the underlying epidemic structure or merely approximate it. In this context, Del Ponte (2026) introduced the concept of the functional epidemic phenotype, proposing that resistance should be defined based on the temporal dynamics of disease development rather than on static or aggregated measures (Seidel et al., 2026). Under this framework, disease progress curves are treated as functional objects, and similarities among genotypes are quantified through distances between their epidemic trajectories (Del Ponte, 2026). These trajectories are typically estimated as genotype-specific mean curves across multiple environments, allowing the underlying resistance phenotype to be characterized while accounting for environmental variability. Consequently, scalar assessments such as final severity can be interpreted as projections of a higher-dimensional functional space defined by the complete epidemic trajectory. In agroecosystems where multiple pathogens co-occur, the development of wheat genotypes which possess resistance to multiple diseases is highly desirable. However, this is often genetically complex, and genomic regions associated with resistance to one disease may also influence responses to others. In some cases, pleiotropic loci such as Lr34/Yr18/Pm38/Bdv1 confer partial resistance to multiple pathogens simultaneously (Krattinger et al., 2009; Ellis et al., 2014), whereas in others, pleiotropic genes with negative trade-offs may arise, with resistance to one disease associated with increased susceptibility to another (Brown and Rant, 2013; Wiesner-Hanks and Nelson, 2016). As a result, it is challenging to identify genotypes that perform consistently well across multiple diseases. In such a context, the problem can be framed as a multi-objective decision problem, for which Pareto front analysis provides an appropriate framework to identify sets of optimal solutions rather than a single best solution. The Pareto front consists of non-dominated solutions, in which no solution is superior across all objectives simultaneously (Srinivas et al., 1994; Amanifard et al., 2008). In multi-objective optimization, three main strategies are commonly described: criterion-based selection, aggregation methods, and Pareto-based selection. While criterion-based and aggregation approaches reduce the problem to a single objective, Pareto-based selection preserves the multi-dimensional nature of the problem by identifying non-dominated solutions that represent trade-offs among objectives (Hajela and C-Y, 1992; Horn, 1997). Despite its conceptual relevance, the application of this framework to characterize multi-disease resistance based on epidemic trajectories of multiple diseases remains largely unexplored. To address this gap, we analyzed disease progress curves of three major wheat foliar diseases—LR, PM, and TS—across multiple environments. Specifically, we asked: (i) whether functional clustering of disease progress curves identifies distinct resistance phenotypes; (ii) how these phenotypes correspond to final-severity-based classifications; (iii) whether simplified phenological checkpoints can approximate trajectory-based classification; and (iv) whether functional distances derived from epidemic trajectories can be integrated across diseases to identify genotypes combining resistance to multiple foliar diseases. Materials and Method Data source . Multi-environment assessments for LR, PM and TS were conducted from 2019 to 2025 across five municipalities in Paraná State and one municipality in São Paulo State (Itaberá), Brazil. These locations represent major wheat-producing regions of southeastern Paraná state, with Itaberá included due to its proximity to the state border. Environments were defined as unique municipality × year combinations. In Itaberá, however, rainfed and irrigated trials, conducted side by side, were considered distinct environments. Within each environment, field experiments were conducted to evaluate the resistance of 33 to 60 wheat cultivars (depending on the environment) to the LR, PM and TS diseases under fungicide-treated and non-treated conditions. In each experiment, disease severity, for each disease, was assessed repeatedly for each genotype–disease combination, and the corresponding crop developmental stage was recorded using the Zadoks growth scale at each assessment. Initially, the dataset was filtered separately for each disease to retain only genotypes evaluated in at least three years and four municipalities. In addition, only environments in which final disease severity exceeded 10% for at least one genotype were considered. After applying these disease-specific filters, a common set of cultivars was selected across all three diseases. From the initial 192 genotypes, 25 cultivars were retained for analysis based on the interest of the collaborating researchers and the availability of a larger number of field experiments. The final selection also ensured a minimum of five observations over time per genotype–environment combination, with the number of environments per cultivar ranging from 5 to 41, depending on the disease . The final dataset included 628 fitted curves for TS, 472 for LR, and 750 for PM (Table S1) . Analyses . Following Del Ponte (2026), a hierarchical beta-regression generalized additive model (HGAM) (Pedersen et al. 2019) was fitted to characterize the nonlinear progression of disease severity over time for each genotype while accounting for environmental variability. Disease severity, expressed as a proportion bounded between 0 and 1, was modeled assuming a Beta distribution with a logit link function. Because the Beta distribution is defined on the open interval (0,1), severity values were transformed prior to model fitting following the approach of Smithson and Verkuilen (2006) to accommodate potential boundary values (0 or 1). Disease progress curves were aligned along a phenological axis using the Zadoks growth scale (Zadoks et al. 1974). This alignment allows epidemic trajectories to be compared at equivalent biological stages. In this framework, genotype reaction to the disease was represented by the temporal trajectory of disease development rather than scalar summaries. Functional similarity among genotypes was quantified using trajectory-based distance measures computed over the entire disease progress curve (Del Ponte, 2026). These distances quantify dissimilarity among epidemic trajectories and provide a quantitative basis for comparing genotype reaction to disease. Let y ijkt denote the observed disease severity for genotype i in environment j at time t , where 𝑡 represents crop developmental stage expressed according to the Zadoks growth stage (Zadoks et al., 1974). Under the HGAM framework, the expected value of disease severity, μ ijkt was modeled as: y ijkt ~ Beta( μ​ ijkt ,ϕ) (1) logit μ​ ijkt =β0​+ β i ​​+f(t)+ f i (t)+ b j (2) where, the distribution of the response variable, assuming disease severity follows a Beta distribution with mean μ ​ ijkt and precision parameter ϕ (1). The β 0 is the intercept, β i represents the parametric effect of genotype i , f(t) is a global smooth function describing the overall epidemic trajectory over time, f i (t) represents genotype-specific smooth deviation capturing differences in disease progression among genotypes, and b j ~ Ν(0, σ j 2 ) is a random effect accounting for environmental heterogeneity (2). Smooth functions were represented using penalized regression splines. The global temporal trend was modeled using a spline basis with dimension 𝑘 =10, while genotype-specific smooths of time were fitted with basis dimension k=4, allowing flexibility in epidemic trajectory shape while controlling model complexity. To further reduce the risk of overfitting, an additional smoothing penalty was applied using a gamma inflation factor (γ = 1.6). From the fitted model, environment-adjusted genotype mean trajectories were predicted on a common time grid spanning the epidemic period. Pairwise functional differences among genotype trajectories were quantified using the L ² norm computed over the time domain. Specifically, the distance between two predicted trajectories μ a (t) and μ b (t) was calculated as: d ab = k=1 T μ a t k - μ b t k 2 ∆t (3) Where, d ab is the functional distance between the predicted disease progress trajectories of genotypes a and b; μ a t k and μ b t k are the predicted disease severities for genotypes a and b at time point t k ; T is the total number of points in the common time grid used to evaluate the trajectories; and ∆t represents the time interval between consecutive points in the grid. The resulting functional distance matrix was used for hierarchical clustering based on Ward’s minimum variance method (ward.D2) to identify groups of genotypes exhibiting similar epidemic dynamics. To formally evaluate whether the resulting trajectory groups differed in epidemic behavior, a distance-based permutation test was performed on the curve-level L ² distance matrix using 999 permutations. When appropriate, permutations were restricted within experimental strata to preserve the original experimental design structure. Model parameters were estimated using fast restricted maximum likelihood (fREML) implemented in the compare_curves() function of the {r4pde} package in R (Del Ponte, 2026b). After the trajectory groups were identified, each functional group was labeled using the same resistance categories adopted in the annual classifications provided by the researchers responsible for the field experiments. To compare functional classification with a scalar endpoint approach while minimizing differences due to the statistical procedure, an analogous beta-regression GAM was fitted using final disease severity as the response variable. Final severity was defined as the last disease assessment available for each environment × genotype combination. The model included genotype as a fixed effect and environment as a random effect, following the same distributional framework used in the functional analysis. Estimated marginal means for each genotype were obtained on the response scale, converted to percentages, and used to compute pairwise distances among genotypes. These distances were submitted to hierarchical clustering using Ward’s minimum variance method To interpret biological differences among clusters, epidemiologically meaningful features were extracted from fitted curves, including time required to reach 5% disease severity ( t 5 ) and maximum epidemic velocity ( v max ). The t 5 was also derived from the fitted curves and used as an indicator of epidemic onset. The v max was calculated as the maximum first derivative of the fitted disease progress curve, representing the rate of increase in disease severity (proportion) per unit of crop developmental stage according to the Zadoks scale. To assess whether epidemic groups differed in these dynamic parameters, the Kruskal–Wallis test was used to compare t 5 ​ and v max among groups. Because the variables did not necessarily meet normality assumptions, non-parametric methods were adopted. Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient was used to evaluate associations among epidemic onset ( t 5 ), epidemic velocity ( v max ), and final disease severity. Mean final severity was calculated for each genotype and used in the correlation analyses. To integrate resistance across diseases, functional distances were used to derive a multi-disease resistance index. For each disease, the distance of each cultivar to the resistant group was calculated as the mean functional distance between that cultivar and the cultivars belonging to the resistant cluster. If the resistant group contains k cultivars, the distance for cultivar i was defined as: d i = 1 k r=1 k D(i,r) (4) Where D(i,r) represents the pairwise functional distance between cultivar i and cultivar r in the resistant cluster. Smaller values of d i indicate greater similarity to the resistant ideotype. When genotype i also belonged to the resistant cluster, the self-distance was excluded from the calculation. To allow comparisons across diseases, the distances were standardized using z-scores. For each disease, standardized values were calculated as: Z i,d = d i,d - μ d σ d (5) Where d i,d is the distance of cultivar i for disease d , and μ d and σ d are the mean and standard deviation of distances across all genotypes for that disease. For each genotype, the mean standardized distance across the three diseases was then calculated to represent overall resistance performance. To account for potential imbalance in resistance among diseases, the standard deviation of the three standardized distances was computed and used as a penalty term. The final multi-disease resistance index was therefore defined as the mean standardized distance plus a weighted penalty proportional to the between-disease variability. To complement the composite resistance index, a Pareto front analysis was performed using the disease-specific distances to the resistant cluster. For each genotype x , a vector d(x)=((d1​(x),d2​(x),d3​(x)) was defined, where each component represents the distance to the resistant cluster for a given disease. Because smaller distances indicate greater similarity to the resistant phenotype, the analysis was formulated as a minimization problem. A genotype x a was said to dominate genotype x b if d i ​( x a ​) ≤ d i ​( x b ​) for all diseases i with strict inequality for at least one disease j (Coello et al., 2007). Genotypes not dominated by any other were classified as belonging to the Pareto front, representing non-dominated solutions in the multi-disease resistance space and reflecting optimal trade-offs among resistance responses to the different diseases. Results For all three diseases, the number of functional groups obtained was consistent with the number of resistance classes annually defined by researchers in the field experiments, which are used to guide cultivar recommendations to farmers: TS (k = 3), and PM and LR (k = 4) ( Table 1; Figures 1; 2; 3 (A,B)). However, when genotypes were grouped using functional distances, their classifications did not fully agree with those based on final disease severity, and the level of agreement varied among diseases. For TS, 13 of the 25 genotypes were assigned to the same groups by both the functional and final-severity-based classifications (Table 2, Figure S1A). This correspondence resulted in an adjusted Rand index (ARI) of 0.36 and a Cramér’s V association measure of 0.65. Cluster validation metrics further indicated a satisfactory structure of the functional groups, with a silhouette value of 0.54 and a cluster R² of 0.86, suggesting that genotypes were reasonably well allocated within their respective clusters (Table 1). A similar temporal disease progression was observed for genotypes classified as moderately resistant (MR) and moderately susceptible (MS), which may contribute to the difficulty in clearly separating these resistance classes (Figure 1A). In susceptible cultivars, disease symptoms were first detected around Zadoks growth stage 35, and final disease severity in this group ranged approximately from 15% to 25%. Based on the disease progress curves along the Zadoks scale, a single TS disease assessment during the growing season would most effectively differentiate genotypes if conducted between Zadoks stages 65 and 75, or later in the season after Zadoks 85 (Figure 1E). When two assessments are performed, improved discrimination among genotypes can be achieved by evaluating at approximately Zadoks 40 followed by a second assessment around Zadoks 70 (Figure 1F). For LR, the functional and final-severity-based classifications agreed for 23 of the 25 genotypes (Table 2, Figure S1B). The ARI was 0.76 and Cramér’s V was 0.92, while cluster validation metrics still indicated a well-defined structure (silhouette = 0.55; cluster R² = 0.94). Disease progression patterns overlapped among adjacent resistance classes throughout much of the crop cycle. Clearer differentiation among R and MR classes emerged later in the season, particularly after Zadoks stage 80, when MR and R genotypes showed greater separation in disease severity (Figure 2A). In susceptible cultivars, disease symptoms were first detected around Zadoks growth stage 50, and final disease severity in this group ranged approximately from 30% to 35%. Consistent with this pattern, ARI values across growth stages indicated that a single disease assessment performed after Zadoks 80 would maximize the likelihood of distinguishing resistance classes (Figure 2E). When two assessments are conducted, the stages providing the greatest genotype discrimination, based on the area under the curve (AUC) analysis, correspond to approximately Zadoks 67 for the first evaluation and Zadoks 77 for the second (Figure 2F). For PM, the functional and final-severity-based classifications differed for only one genotype (Table 2, Figure S1C). Disease progression curves showed a clearer separation among resistance classes over time, with increases in severity occurring at earlier Zadoks growth stages (Figure 3A). The ARI was 0.36 and Cramér’s V was 0.94. Cluster validation metrics indicated a well-defined cluster structure, with a cluster R² of 0.97 and a silhouette value of 0.71. In susceptible cultivars, disease symptoms were first detected around Zadoks growth stage 20, and final disease severity in this group was approximately 60%. Analysis of ARI values across growth stages indicated that a single disease assessment performed between Zadoks stages 70 and 80 provided t he highest agreement between classifications (ARI &gt; 0.75) (Figure 3E). When two assessments are conducted, the stages that maximize genotype discrimination correspond to approximately Zadoks 54 for the first evaluation and Zadoks 70 for the second (Figure 3F). For all diseases, the Kruskal–Wallis test indicated significant differences among the groups defined by the functional clustering approach for both the time required to reach 5% disease severity ( t 5 ) and the epidemic rate ( v max ) (p &lt; 0.01). Susceptible genotypes exhibited earlier symptom onset and higher epidemic rates compared to more resistant genotypes. Multiple linear regression analysis showed that epidemic dynamics explained a large proportion of the variation in final disease severity among genotypes. The t 5 had a significant negative effect, indicating that later epidemic onset was associated with lower final severity (Figures 1C, 2C and 3C). In contrast, the v max showed a significant positive effect on final severity (Figures 1D, 2D and 3D). A large group of genotypes exhibited low mean standardized distances to the resistant cluster (mean &lt; 0); however, only four—TBIO Talisma (22), TBIO Toruk (24), TBIO Titan (23), and TBIO Calibre (16)—combined low distances with low between-disease variability, indicating more stable multi-disease resistance (Figure 4A). In the ternary plot, proximity to a vertex indicates greater resistance to that disease, whereas central positions reflect more balanced resistance (Figure 4B). Genotypes 16, 22, 24, 23, along with ORS Feroz (7), ORS Turbo(12), TBIO Motriz (19), Bar 10 (1) and ORS 1403 (6), showed the most balanced profiles and were considered part of Pareto front. In contrast, genotypes TBIO Duque (18) and TBIO Blanc (15) were mainly associated with TS resistance; ORS Madre Perola (8), TBIO Audaz (14), and TBIO Capaz (17) with LR and TS; TBIO Ponteiro (20) with PM and TS; and TBIO Aton (13), ORS Senna (10), ORS Selvagem (9), BRS Sanhaco (3), BRS Gralha Azul (2) and BRS TR133 (4) with PM and LR. Discussion Functional clustering of disease progress curves revealed that the correspondence between trajectory-based resistance phenotypes and final-severity-based classifications varied among wheat diseases. The largest discrepancy occurred for TS, indicating that final severity alone may not fully capture differences in epidemic trajectories for this disease. Conversely, PM and LR showed strong agreement between functional and final-severity-based classifications, confirming that late-season assessments are generally adequate for representing genotype resistance patterns in these pathosystems. Under the practical constraints of field phenotyping, identifying optimal Zadoks growth stages can therefore improve approximation of trajectory-based resistance, particularly for TS, while reinforcing the value of late assessments for PM and LR. In addition, integrating functional distances across diseases within a multi-objective framework allowed the identification of genotypes combining resistance to multiple foliar diseases. When the entire disease progress curve was considered through functional distances, the classification of cultivar reactions to TS differed from final-severity-based ratings for most cultivars. Among the cultivars with inconsistent classifications, discrepancies occurred mainly in the intermediate resistance categories, particularly moderately resistant (MR) and moderately susceptible (MS). In many cases, cultivars classified as MR or MS based on final severity were assigned to more susceptible functional groups, indicating that final severity tended to overestimate their resistance. This pattern reflects the intermediate nature of these categories. For example, cultivars classified as MS showed some ability to slow epidemic development compared with highly susceptible (HS) cultivars, but this reduction in disease progress was not sufficient for them to be functionally grouped with MR cultivars. Therefore, although scalar measures such as final severity may place these cultivars in intermediate classes, they may not fully capture differences in the timing, rate, and shape of epidemic development. In contrast, functional distance metrics capture differences across multiple phases of the epidemic by comparing entire disease progress trajectories. Thus, two epidemics with similar final severity or cumulative disease burden may still differ markedly in their temporal patterns, resulting in distinct functional distances and, consequently, different resistance classifications (Del Ponte, 2026). The magnitude of differences in epidemic rate among resistance classes may explain why the agreement between final-severity-based and functional classifications differed among diseases. For LR and PM, cultivar classes differed more markedly in epidemic velocity, with maximum progression rates ranging from approximately 0 to 0.04 for LR and from 0 to 0.02 for PM. These stronger differences in disease progress were also reflected in final severity, allowing clearer separation among resistance classes and resulting in higher agreement between final-severity-based and functional classifications. In contrast, TS showed a much narrower range of maximum epidemic rates, from approximately 0.003 to 0.009, and final severity differences among neighboring resistance classes were less pronounced. Under these conditions, final severity alone provided limited resolution for separating TS resistance phenotypes. By comparing the full disease progress curves, functional distance captured subtle but consistent differences in epidemic trajectories that were not fully expressed in final severity, enabling a more refined classification of cultivar reactions to TS. Discrepancies between final-severity-based classifications (FS) and functional classifications (FC) were more likely to occur among neighboring resistance classes, such as HS–MS, MS–MR, or MR–R. Importantly, discrepancies spanning more than one resistance class were not observed, indicating that functional classification did not produce major reassignments but rather refined the interpretation of adjacent categories. However, even shifts between neighboring classes may be biologically and practically relevant. In breeding programs, a genotype classified as MS rather than MR, or MR rather than R, may be selected or discarded depending on the breeding objective. Therefore, subtle differences in epidemic behavior captured by functional distances may reveal resistance components that are not fully expressed in final severity but could still have genetic value, such as slower epidemic development, delayed disease onset, or reduced maximum progression rate. However, these results should not be interpreted as direct cultivar recommendations for the following growing season. Because the models were fitted across multiple years and locations, the functional classifications represent environment-adjusted mean resistance patterns rather than performance under a specific pathogen population, location, or year. Field recommendations should continue to rely on recent regional performance, as cultivar reactions can vary substantially across years and locations depending on host–pathogen interactions and environmental conditions. Therefore, the primary value of the functional approach is to support breeding programs by improving resistance phenotyping and identifying genotypes with more desirable epidemic trajectories over time. The classification of cultivars were closely associated with key epidemiological components, including the timing of epidemic onset ( t5 ) and the rate of disease progression ( vmax ). Cultivars classified as resistant exhibited a delayed onset of symptoms, indicating a reduction in effective initial inoculum and a consequent delay in epidemic development. This pattern is consistent with the action of vertical resistance, which reduces initial inoculum by eliminating avirulent races from the pathogen population (Vanderplank, 1968). In addition, more resistant cultivars also showed lower values of vmax , indicating a reduced rate of disease progression. In contrast to vertical resistance, horizontal resistance acts by reducing the rate of pathogen development and is at least partially effective against all pathogen races (Vanderplank, 1968). Because epidemic behavior depends on the resistance components expressed by the cultivar, temporal delays in epidemic can mitigate yield losses, even when final severities are similar. Genotypes are evaluated in multi-environment trials to identify those with stable performance and broad adaptation, and disease assessments are often limited to one or two time points due to logistical constraints (Zhang et al., 2007; Losert et al., 2017; Francki et al., 2020; Pour-Aboughadareh et al., 2022). Although assessment timing may vary, evaluations are generally conducted at later growth stages under the assumption that adult plant resistance is more fully expressed, even though it can often be detected in the late tillering stage (Zadoks 29) and becomes more effective during the booting stage (Zadoks 40-49) (Zhang et al., 2007; Shlıbak and Zencirci, 2021; Dinglasan et al., 2021; Merrick et al., 2022). Although functional analyses provide a more complete representation of epidemic development, their implementation requires repeated disease assessments over time, which may not always be feasible in breeding programs. In this context, the optimal assessment windows identified here may provide a practical alternative by helping breeders prioritize the growth stages that best capture differences in epidemic behavior among genotypes. Although powdery mildew (PM) initiates early, the most informative stage for discrimination was between Zadoks 70–80. Leaf rust (LR) was best discriminated after Zadoks 80, while tan spot (TS) showed optimal discrimination between Zadoks 65–75. Although these single-time assessments do not represent the entire epidemic trajectory, they indirectly capture key epidemic features because the optimal timing was derived from functional disease progress curves. Therefore, these stages correspond to the points at which differences in epidemic trajectories among genotypes become sufficiently pronounced to allow reliable discrimination among resistance classes, particularly between neighboring categories such as HS–MS, MS–MR, and MR–R. Assessment at non-optimal stages may lead to misclassification of cultivar reactions and may reduce the reliability of information used to support breeding decisions and, indirectly, cultivar recommendation systems, as observed for TS. Disease pressure was considerably higher for PM and LR than for TS, highlighting the need for increased attention to the pathogens that cause these diseases. This is particularly important given the biological and dispersal-related characteristics of Blumeria graminis f. sp. tritici and Puccinia triticina , including high mutation rates, regular sexual reproduction (for B . graminis f. sp. Tritici ), large effective population sizes, and efficient airborne dispersal, which enable these pathogens to rapidly overcome deployed race-specific resistance genes and make them a major focus of research and breeding efforts (Kolmer, 2005; Singh et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2021). In our study, PM reached approximately 60% severity in susceptible genotypes, making it the most severe disease observed. In Paraná state (Brazil), where the data were collected, PM has become the most prevalent wheat disease since 2015, likely due to mild and humid winters and the widespread use of susceptible cultivars. In addition, pathogen populations in Brazil have exhibited high virulence complexity toward Pm resistance genes, with isolates capable of overcoming up to 85% of previously deployed resistance sources (Kloppe et al., 2022). Although assessing the resistance durability or temporal changes in cultivar reaction was beyond the scope of our study, this scenario raises the hypothesis that resistance phenotypes may not remain stable across years and locations. Statistical approaches for genotype stability could be used in future studies to test whether cultivars maintain consistent resistance responses over time and space, particularly under recurrent PM epidemics and changing pathogen populations (Finlay and Wilkinson, 1963; Francis and Kannenberg, 1978). As PM is typically one of the first fungal diseases to appear in wheat, its rapid and polycyclic development under favorable conditions can lead to early and sustained epidemic buildup. Consequently, effective management requires integrated strategies, particularly those targeting early infections to improve control efficiency (Lau et al., 2020). An important aspect for continued breeding progress is the availability of resistance to multiple diseases (Krattinger et al., 2013; Losert et al., 2017). Considering the multi-objective analysis, a group of seventeenth genotypes was identified as belonging to the Pareto front, representing the best trade-offs in resistance across the three diseases. Among these, four genotypes (TBIO Talisma, TBIO Toruk, TBIO Titan, and TBIO Calibre — exhibited low variability (&lt;0.5) in resistance across diseases, indicating a more balanced resistance profile. Given that higher disease severity was observed in more susceptible genotypes for LR and PM, prioritizing resistance to these diseases appears to be a practical strategy. In this context, genotypes TBIO Aton, ORS Senna, ORS Selvagem, BRS Sanhaco, BRS Gralha Azul and BRS TR133 emerge as promising candidates for breeding programs. However, final selection may depend on the desired balance of resistance across diseases, particularly when TS is also considered. Not surprisingly, most genotypes resistant to LR also exhibited resistance to PM. This pattern may be associated with the presence of pleiotropic resistance genes such as Lr34 ( = Yr18/Sr57/Pm38), Lr46 ( = Yr29/Sr58/Pm39), and Lr67 ( = Yr46/Sr55/Pm46), which confer partial resistance to multiple fungal pathogens, including those causing LR and PM (Krattinger et al., 2009; Ellis et al., 2014). Among these, Lr34 is one of the most widely deployed resistance genes in wheat breeding and has remained effective for over a century despite its extensive use (Kolmer et al., 2008; Krattinger et al., 2013; 2019). Environmental variability was accounted for by including environment as a random intercept, allowing the overall level of disease severity to vary among environments while assuming a common temporal trajectory structure. This approach reduces confounding effects of the environment and increases the likelihood of selecting genotypes based on their genetic resistance rather than environmental influence. This is particularly important given that resistance to some diseases may be environmentally unstable (Jighly et al., 2016). In addition to accounting for environmental effects, our approach incorporates key epidemiological components that characterize resistance, capturing disease development over time rather than relying solely on end-point assessments. This framework minimizes the loss of epidemiological information and provides a more detailed and biologically meaningful basis for phenotypic characterization of genotype reaction to disease than scalar measures. Functional approaches do not replace traditional scalar measures, but complement them by integrating temporal disease dynamics (Seidel et al. 2026; Del Ponte 2026). This results in a more biologically meaningful representation of resistance, improving genotype classification and supporting more informed decisions in breeding and disease management.
